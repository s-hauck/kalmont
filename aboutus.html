About Us

Our Mission

At Kalmont our mission is to provide rapid, skilled, and compassionate search and rescue services in times of crisis. Committed to saving lives, we operate with professionalism, teamwork, and resilience, ensuring the safety of those in need—whether in the wilderness, mountains, urban environments, or at sea. Through continuous training, cutting-edge technology, and collaboration with local and national agencies, we strive to respond swiftly and effectively to emergencies. Our team stands ready 24/7 to serve our community with integrity, courage, and an unwavering dedication to bringing people home.

Who are our members?

Our search and rescue crew is an elite team of highly trained professionals, each forged through years of experience in some of the harshest and most demanding environments on Earth. Comprising climbers, alpinists, military veterans, wilderness survival experts, paramedics, and specialized rescue operators, our team is built on a foundation of grit, skill, and unyielding dedication to saving lives. Every member undergoes intensive training that pushes them beyond their limits—learning advanced rope rescue techniques, helicopter extractions, swift-water operations, avalanche response, and emergency medical care in extreme conditions. They are no strangers to adversity, having spent years honing their craft on sheer rock faces, navigating treacherous alpine terrain, and enduring the unpredictable elements of the wilderness. Whether it’s scaling vertical cliffs to reach stranded climbers, navigating through blizzards to locate missing hikers, or diving into raging waters for a swift-water rescue, our team thrives in situations where others cannot. What makes them truly exceptional is not just their physical ability but their unwavering mental toughness, their ability to make split-second life-or-death decisions, and their commitment to risking everything to bring people home. When disaster strikes, we are the ones who answer the call—no matter the conditions, no matter the challenge.

What equipment do we use?

With a team composed of elite professionals from backgrounds in alpine climbing, backcountry skiing, military service, and technical rescue, there are few situations we aren’t prepared for. Our diverse skill set and specialized training allow us to execute complex operations in nearly any environment, from rugged mountain peaks to swiftwater rapids and dense forest terrain. We conduct long-line helicopter rescues, allowing us to extract individuals from sheer cliffs, avalanche paths, and inaccessible wilderness areas with speed and precision. Our high-angle rope rescue teams are capable of executing technical extractions from deep crevasses, rock faces, and unstable terrain where traditional methods fall short. In extreme cold environments, our glacier and alpine rescue specialists navigate dangerous ice fields, avalanche zones, and high-altitude conditions to reach those in distress. Beyond the mountains, we excel in swiftwater and ice rescues, using specialized gear and training to perform operations in fast-moving rivers, frozen lakes, and flood zones. When conditions turn unpredictable, our wilderness search teams utilize GPS tracking, searchlights, drones, and K9 units to locate missing persons in even the most remote areas. With the talent and expertise on our team, there is no mission too demanding, no terrain too extreme, and no conditions too harsh. Whether it's a high-altitude rescue, a flood evacuation, or an off-grid medical emergency, we are always ready to answer the call.

Avalanche

Avalanches can happen whenever there is snow lying on the ground at a sufficient angle. Every year there are avalanche accidents in Whistler. While there is always an avalanche risk in winter, some of these accidents could have been avoided by awareness and planning.

Hypothermia

The two most common causes of hypothermia on the hill are exhaustion and injury. Walkers with inadequate food, clothing and shelter will not be able to sustain the level of physical work to replace the heat they are losing. Injury leads to immobility and a similar inability to generate heat – just think how you cool, off when you stop for your 'piece'.
