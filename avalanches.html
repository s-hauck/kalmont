Avalanches

What is an avalanche?

An avalanche is a rapid flow of snow, ice, and debris down a mountainside, often triggered by factors like heavy snowfall, warming temperatures, or human activity. Avalanches occur when a weak layer of snow collapses under the weight of the snow above it, causing a sudden and potentially destructive slide. They range from small sluffs to massive, fast-moving slabs capable of burying everything in their path. Avalanches are a major hazard in mountainous regions, making awareness, proper safety measures, and rescue training essential for skiers, snowboarders, and mountaineers.

Who needs to look out for it?

Avalanches pose a serious risk to anyone venturing into mountainous terrain, especially backcountry skiers, snowboarders, mountaineers, snowmobilers, and hikers. These groups are most at risk because they often travel on steep, snow-covered slopes where avalanches are more likely to occur. However, avalanches can also impact mountain communities, infrastructure, and highways, threatening residents, workers, and travelers in alpine regions. Anyone who spends time in avalanche-prone areas should stay informed about conditions, carry proper safety gear (like a beacon, probe, and shovel), and know how to assess and mitigate risks.

What are some warning signs?

To recognize avalanche danger, watch for key warning signs in the terrain and snowpack. Recent heavy snowfall, rapid temperature changes, or strong winds can create unstable conditions. Cracks in the snow, a "whumpfing" sound (indicating snow settling), or visible slabs breaking away are telltale signs of instability. Steep slopes (30–45 degrees) are prime avalanche terrain, especially if there are signs of recent slides. Be cautious after rain or rapid warming, as these can weaken the snowpack. Always check avalanche forecasts, test snow stability, and be prepared with proper safety gear before venturing into avalanche-prone areas.

What to do if you set one off?

If you trigger an avalanche, try to escape to the side of the slide immediately. If you’re caught, attempt to stay on the surface by swimming and kicking to keep from being buried too deeply. Before being fully engulfed, create an air pocket by covering your mouth with your arm and expanding your chest. Once the snow settles, stay calm and conserve oxygen—yelling won’t help, as sound is muffled under snow. If you're with others, they should immediately begin a rescue using a beacon, probe, and shovel. Since survival chances drop significantly after 15 minutes, quick action is crucial.

Hypothermia

The two most common causes of hypothermia on the hill are exhaustion and injury. Walkers with inadequate food, clothing and shelter will not be able to sustain the level of physical work to replace the heat they are losing. Injury leads to immobility and a similar inability to generate heat – just think how you cool, off when you stop for your 'piece'.
