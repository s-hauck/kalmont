Hypothermia

Prepare for the cold

What is hypothermia?

Hypothermia is a dangerous condition that occurs when the body’s core temperature drops below 95°F (35°C). This can happen when someone is exposed to cold environments for extended periods, especially if they are wet, windy, or not wearing proper clothing. As the body loses heat faster than it can produce, vital organs like the heart, brain, and lungs can begin to function poorly, leading to life-threatening effects.

How can it affect you?

Hypothermia poses serious risks to the body if left untreated. As the body’s core temperature drops, it can impair the functioning of vital organs, leading to confusion, difficulty moving, and loss of coordination. In severe cases, hypothermia can cause unconsciousness, organ failure, and even death. If not addressed promptly, hypothermia can rapidly worsen, and individuals may not be able to recognize the severity of their situation.

How can we prevent it?

To prevent hypothermia, it’s important to stay warm, dry, and prepared when exposed to cold conditions. Dress in layers, including moisture-wicking base layers, insulating mid-layers, and a waterproof outer layer to protect against wind and rain. Avoid cotton, as it retains moisture and can lead to heat loss. Stay dry by wearing appropriate gear, and take breaks in sheltered areas to warm up. Keep moving to maintain circulation, but avoid exhaustion, which can increase your vulnerability. Always carry extra clothing, and if possible, have access to shelter or a heat source. In extreme cold, plan your activities to minimize exposure and never underestimate the risks of hypothermia.

What to do if you encounter it?

If you suspect someone has hypothermia, it’s crucial to act quickly. First, move the person to a warmer, sheltered environment to stop further heat loss. Remove any wet clothing and replace it with dry, insulating layers. Gradually warm the body with blankets, sleeping bags, or by sharing body heat, but avoid using direct heat sources like hot water or heating pads, as this can cause shock. Offer warm, non-alcoholic drinks if the person is conscious and able to swallow. Avoid letting them walk or move too much, as this can worsen circulation problems. Seek medical help immediately, as severe hypothermia requires professional treatment.
